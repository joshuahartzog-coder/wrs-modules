Integrity: when the right call costs you something

Applied Short Module · 8–12 min Standard 1.1.2 — Integrity Use: Opener / closer / exam prep

Applied integrity isn't about knowing that honesty matters — you already know that. It's about navigating the situations where honesty is harder: where loyalty, convenience, self-protection, or authority push against it. This module focuses on those harder cases.

Applied integrity questions often involve a multi-step situation: a mistake is made, and then a choice about whether to disclose it. Or a coworker or supervisor pressures you to do something questionable. The correct answer almost always involves transparency and raising concerns through proper channels — not compliance with an unethical request, and not unilateral action that bypasses the chain of command. Watch for options that frame silence or complicity as loyalty.

The judgment layer

Applied integrity scenarios tend to involve one of three tensions:

  • Loyalty vs. honesty — a colleague asks you to cover for them, stay quiet about something, or vouch for something untrue.
  • Self-protection vs. accountability — you made a mistake. Disclosing it will be uncomfortable. Staying quiet might let it slide.
  • Authority vs. ethics — a supervisor instructs you to do something you know is wrong. Following orders feels safer than pushing back.

In all three cases, the professional standard is the same: be transparent, raise concerns through the appropriate channel, and don't let social pressure override your ethical judgment.

Exam-style scenario — try it first

Avery is a team lead. She realizes a report she submitted last week contained an error — a significant one that could affect the project's direction. Her supervisor hasn't noticed yet. Avery could quietly correct the error in the next version and say nothing. Or she could tell her supervisor now, explain the mistake, and deal with the fallout.

What should Avery do?

Avery should disclose the error now. Her supervisor is making decisions based on that report. Staying silent means her supervisor continues operating on bad information — which is an integrity failure that compounds the original mistake. The professional move is to bring it to her supervisor directly: "I found an error in the report I submitted. Here's what it was and here's the corrected version." That's accountability. Hoping no one notices is not.

⭐ Optional extender — if you finish early
At what point does protecting a coworker become being complicit in their dishonesty? Where's the line, and how would you explain it to the coworker when you decline to help?
The line is active participation. Staying quiet when you observe something questionable is a gray area. Actively lying, vouching for false information, or helping someone cover up a problem is complicity — you're now part of the deception. To the coworker: "I'm not going to report you, but I also can't back you up if anyone asks. You need to get ahead of this yourself." That's not betrayal — that's the honest version of looking out for someone.
