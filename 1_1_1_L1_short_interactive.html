What does a good work ethic actually look like?

L1 Foundations Short module · 8–12 min Standard 1.1.1 — Positive Work Ethic Use: Opener or closer

What you need to know

Work ethic is how you show up — not just physically, but mentally. Employers across every industry consistently say it is the number one thing they look for and the number one reason they let people go.

A positive work ethic has three parts:

  • Attendance and punctuality — coming every day and arriving on time. Being late or absent affects your coworkers, not just your supervisor.
  • Willingness to take direction — receiving feedback or instructions without arguing, making excuses, or shutting down. This is not about being a pushover — it's about being coachable.
  • Motivation to accomplish the task — staying on task, working independently when needed, and following through. Employers call this being "self-directed."

Here's the honest truth: skills can be taught. Work ethic is much harder to teach. That is why employers value it so highly — and why they notice it quickly when it is missing.

Workplace scenario

Marcus and Deja both work at a hotel front desk. Marcus arrives exactly on time most days, does his assigned tasks well, and leaves at the end of his shift. Deja arrives five minutes early, notices when a coworker is overwhelmed and helps without being asked, and follows up on guest requests even when she could pass them off. Six months later, Deja is offered a lead position. Marcus is surprised.

What made the difference? Both had good attendance. Both did their jobs. But Deja demonstrated motivation beyond the minimum — a key part of a strong work ethic.
