Integrity: doing the right thing when no one is watching

Foundational 45–60 min Standard 1.1.2 — Integrity Extended Module
Suggested timing: Warm-up 5 min · Instruction 15 min · Activity 20 min · Quiz + Reflection 10 min

🕐 Warm-up · 5 min — What integrity actually means

Most people have a general sense of what integrity means: being honest, doing the right thing. But in a professional context, integrity is more specific — and harder than it sounds.

Here's the challenge: integrity isn't tested when doing the right thing is easy. It's tested when telling the truth is uncomfortable, when cutting corners would save you time, or when no one would ever find out anyway. What you do in those moments is your integrity.

Nevada's Workplace Readiness Standards define integrity as demonstrating honesty, trustworthiness, and ethical behavior in all professional situations. That includes:

  • Honesty — telling the truth even when it's inconvenient or makes you look bad
  • Accountability — owning your mistakes instead of deflecting or minimizing
  • Ethical decision-making — choosing the right action over the easy one
  • Trustworthiness — following through on commitments; being reliable
  • Confidentiality — handling sensitive information appropriately
BehaviorWhat it signals to employersWhat the absence signals
HonestyYou can be trusted with information and responsibility."I have to double-check everything they tell me."

🕐 Instruction · 15 min — What integrity looks like at work

Integrity failures in the workplace rarely look dramatic. They're usually small decisions that accumulate:

  • You break something and don't tell anyone.
  • You fudge your hours by a few minutes on a slow day.
  • You let a coworker take the blame for something that was partly your fault.
  • You share something a coworker told you in confidence.
  • You see something wrong and stay quiet because it's not your problem.
Industry voice: "We can teach skills. We can't teach someone to be honest. If I can't trust a person to tell me the truth when something goes wrong, I can't use them in a role that matters." — Manufacturing supervisor, Reno NV

The professional standard isn't just about avoiding dishonesty. It's about building a reputation as someone who can be trusted. That reputation takes time to build and very little time to damage.

Marcus is working in a healthcare facility and accidentally drops a piece of equipment, cracking the casing. It still seems to work. His supervisor is in a meeting. No one else saw it happen. He could put it back and say nothing — and probably nothing would happen.

Discuss: What should Marcus do, and why? What are the possible consequences if he says nothing and the equipment later fails?

Professional answer: Marcus should report the incident to his supervisor as soon as the meeting ends — or leave a written note if necessary. In healthcare, equipment integrity is directly tied to patient safety. "It still seems to work" is not a professional standard. Saying nothing is an integrity failure even if the equipment never causes a problem — because it sets a precedent for what Marcus does when no one is watching.

Priya works in an office where her supervisor mentioned in passing that a coworker, Devon, is on a performance improvement plan. Priya and Devon are friendly. A mutual colleague asks Priya directly: "Is Devon in trouble? I heard something." What should Priya do?

Discuss: What are Priya's obligations here? Does the fact that her supervisor mentioned it casually — not formally — change anything?

Professional answer: Priya should not share what she knows. Personnel matters are confidential regardless of how she found out. The professional response is: "I'm not in a position to share anything about that." The source of the information — casual or formal — doesn't change her obligation to protect it.

🕐 Activity · 20 min — The integrity spectrum

📋 Partner or small group activity

Below are six workplace situations. For each one, decide: Is this clearly a problem? A gray area? Or actually fine? Be ready to explain your reasoning.

  1. You're running 5 minutes late and text your supervisor saying you'll be on time.
  2. You made an error on a report but caught it before anyone saw it. You fixed it quietly and didn't mention it.
  3. A customer asks if the product is worth the price. You think it's overpriced, but you say it's great.
  4. Your team is late on a project. In the status meeting, you say "we're on track" to buy more time.
  5. You hear confidential information about a coworker and mention it to your best friend outside of work.
  6. You accidentally overcharge a customer by $5. They don't notice. You keep it because fixing it would take too long.

Discuss your reasoning. Some of these have clear answers. Some are more nuanced. The goal is to identify where the professional line is — and why.
