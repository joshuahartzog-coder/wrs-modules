What employers are actually looking for

Every employer — regardless of industry — is asking three basic questions about every new hire:

  • Will you show up?
  • Will you do what you're asked?
  • Will you stay focused and follow through?

Nevada's Workplace Readiness Standards define a positive work ethic as: coming to work every day on time, a willingness to take direction, and motivation to accomplish the task at hand (Standard 1.1.1).

ComponentWhat it looks likeWhat the absence looks like
Attendance & punctualityArrives on time or early. Gives notice when unable to come in.Frequent late arrivals. Unplanned absences. Leaving early without notice.
Willingness to take directionListens actively. Attempts tasks as instructed. Asks clarifying questions.Argues with supervisors. Ignores feedback. Does tasks their own way without permission.
Motivation to accomplish the taskStays on task. Works independently. Completes work without constant follow-up.Needs repeated reminders. Spends work time on personal activities. Does the minimum.
Industry voice: In surveys of Nevada employers, "reliability and attendance" and "following directions" consistently rank among the top three things employers say new hires struggle with most — above technical skills gaps. Skills can be taught. Work ethic is much harder to teach.

Two employees — same job, different outcomes

Read both scenarios, then discuss the questions below.

Jordan works extremely hard when present and produces excellent results. However, Jordan calls in sick frequently, often arrives late, and responds to supervisor feedback with "I know, but my way works better." Jordan has not been promoted in two years despite strong output.

Taylor is not the highest performer but is always on time, never misses a shift without advance notice, accepts feedback openly, and completes every assigned task before leaving. Taylor was promoted to shift lead after one year.

Taylor's promotion wasn't about raw talent — it was about being dependable.

💬 Class discussion — 15 min (sub: use these prompts to keep the conversation going)
  1. Jordan has better output than Taylor, but Taylor got promoted. Is that fair? Why or why not?
  2. What specifically would Jordan need to change to be considered for promotion?
  3. Think about a job or team you've been part of — which of the three components mattered most in that environment?
  4. Which component do you think is most challenging for someone your age — and why?
  5. What's the difference between a work ethic problem and a skills gap? Can you give an example of each?

Knowledge check

Complete independently. Read the feedback on any wrong answers before moving on.

Question 1 of 5
Carla wants to listen to music while working because she believes it makes her more productive, but her employer does not allow it. What should Carla do?
Question 2 of 5
Hector does his job better than anyone in his department, but he often refuses to help others and never does anything beyond what is assigned. He has never been promoted. Why?
Question 3 of 5
Which of the following is the best example of a work ethic problem — as opposed to a skills gap?
Question 4 of 5
An employee discovers a more efficient method for completing a routine task, but the new method deviates from the company's documented procedure. What is the most professionally appropriate action?
Question 5 of 5 — Scenario
Marco works at a Nevada resort cleaning and resetting guest rooms. His supervisor assigns him six rooms before his shift ends. Halfway through, Marco realizes he needs to leave early to run a personal errand. He has already cleaned three rooms. What should Marco do?
