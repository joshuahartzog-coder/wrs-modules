What integrity actually means at work

Most people think of integrity as simply "being honest." In the workplace, it's broader than that — and harder. Professional integrity means:

  • Honesty — telling the truth even when it's uncomfortable or makes you look bad
  • Accountability — owning your mistakes rather than deflecting or minimizing
  • Ethical decision-making — choosing the right action over the convenient one, especially without supervision
  • Trustworthiness — doing what you say you'll do; being reliable
  • Confidentiality — protecting information that isn't yours to share
Why it matters: Employers consistently say they can train new hires on technical skills but can't teach them to be honest. An employee who can be trusted to tell the truth when something goes wrong — and to fix it — is far more valuable than a technically skilled employee who hides problems.

The hard part: when integrity costs you something

Integrity is easy when doing the right thing is also the convenient thing. The real test is when it isn't:

  • You made a mistake. Admitting it will be uncomfortable.
  • A coworker asks you to cover for them. Saying no might damage the friendship.
  • A supervisor instructs you to do something you know isn't right. Pushing back might create conflict.
  • You know something that should be reported. Reporting it feels like betrayal.

In each of these cases, the professional standard is the same: be transparent, raise concerns through appropriate channels, and don't let social pressure override your ethical judgment.

Jaylen is on a small team that submitted a proposal with an error — an error that Jaylen noticed but didn't flag because he didn't want to slow the process down. The error is now causing problems for the client. His supervisor is trying to figure out what went wrong.

What should Jaylen do now? What are his options, and what are the professional and personal costs of each?

Professional answer: Jaylen needs to come forward. He noticed the error and said nothing — that's an integrity failure, and staying silent now compounds it. The professional move is: "I saw the error before we submitted and didn't flag it. I should have. Here's what I think we can do to address it now." That conversation is hard. But a supervisor who finds out Jaylen knew and stayed quiet will trust him less than if Jaylen had come forward. Integrity means owning the full picture, including your role in what went wrong.

Sofia has been friends with her coworker Dani for two years. Dani asks Sofia to tell their supervisor that Dani was in the office for a full shift last Thursday — when Dani actually left two hours early without permission. Sofia wasn't there either, so she doesn't know for sure what happened. Dani says it's important and that she'll do the same for Sofia someday.

What should Sofia do?

Professional answer: Sofia should decline. Vouching for Dani's whereabouts when she doesn't know what happened is dishonest — and it puts Sofia's own integrity at risk. The right response to Dani is direct: "I can't tell your supervisor I saw you there when I didn't. That's not something I can do." Real friendship doesn't ask someone to compromise their professional reputation. Sofia can be sympathetic without being complicit.

💬 Talk & Discuss

Is there a difference between loyalty and complicity? At what point does protecting a coworker cross into participating in their dishonesty? How would you explain that line to a friend who was asking you to help them out?
