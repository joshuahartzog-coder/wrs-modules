Integrity under pressure: when the right answer isn't obvious

Applied 45–60 min Standard 1.1.2 — Integrity Extended Module
Suggested timing: Warm-up 5 min · Instruction 15 min · Activity 20 min · Quiz + Reflection 10 min

🕐 Warm-up · 5 min — Why integrity gets complicated

The foundational version of integrity is relatively clear: tell the truth, own your mistakes, don't steal. Most people agree on those. Applied integrity is harder — it's about situations where the right answer isn't obvious, where multiple values pull in different directions, or where doing the right thing has real personal cost.

Consider these tensions:

  • Loyalty to a coworker vs. honesty with your supervisor
  • Protecting your own reputation vs. being transparent about a failure
  • Confidentiality vs. speaking up when something seems wrong
  • Being a team player vs. refusing to participate in something unethical

Applied integrity means navigating these tensions — and being able to explain the reasoning behind your choices.

Industry voice: "The test I use is simple: if my name were attached to this decision and anyone could read about it, would I be okay with that? If the answer is no, I don't do it." — Operations manager, Las Vegas NV

🕐 Instruction · 15 min — The hard cases

Three categories of integrity problems appear frequently in the workplace and consistently on the WRS exam:

Situation typeThe integrity challengeThe professional standard
Errors of omissionStaying silent when speaking up is required — letting a false impression stand without actively creating it.Silence that misleads is dishonesty. If you know something relevant, the professional obligation is usually to share it.
Loyalty conflictsA friend or coworker asks you to cover, lie, or look the other way. Protecting them feels like the right thing.Real loyalty doesn't ask someone to compromise their own integrity. Declining protects both of you.
Pressure from aboveA supervisor asks you to do something you know is wrong — cut corners, misrepresent data, treat a customer dishonestly.Following instructions is not a defense for unethical behavior. The professional response is to raise the concern before complying.

Camille works in a food service operation. Her supervisor instructs her to mark items as fresh that were actually prepared the day before — "just this once, we're slammed." Camille knows this violates both company policy and health code. If she says nothing and complies, no one will know. If she pushes back, it could create conflict with a supervisor she otherwise likes.

What should Camille do? Consider the risk, the relationship, and the professional chain of escalation.

Professional answer: Camille should not comply. This isn't a gray area — it's a health code violation and a direct integrity failure. The right step is to say something in the moment: "I'm not comfortable doing that — it's against the health code." If the supervisor pushes back, Camille has a right to escalate to the manager above them. The fact that she was asked to do it doesn't transfer the responsibility.

Derek is preparing a performance report for his team. The actual numbers are below target. His coworkers want to present the data in a way that makes it look better — "frame it positively," they say. They're not asking him to lie exactly — just to emphasize certain figures and leave others out of the summary slide.

What should Derek do? Is "framing" data a form of dishonesty?

Professional answer: Yes, presenting data selectively to create a misleading picture is a form of dishonesty — even if every individual number is accurate. Derek should present the numbers honestly and can include context that explains the challenges the team faced. A supervisor who discovers the real numbers later will trust the team less, not more. Transparency now, while uncomfortable, is the integrity play.

🕐 Activity · 20 min — Case analysis

📋 Small group analysis

For each situation below, identify: (1) what the integrity issue is, (2) what the pressure is to act unethically, and (3) what the professional response is.

  1. A client asks you a question about a product you're not sure about. You give an answer you think sounds right rather than admitting you don't know.
  2. You're in a meeting where your team discusses a decision you think is wrong. You say nothing because you don't want to slow things down.
  3. Your supervisor asks you to shred documents that a new policy requires you to retain. They say it's fine.
  4. A coworker takes credit for your idea in a meeting. Your supervisor compliments them. You say nothing.

Not every situation has a single "right" response — but each has a professional standard. Be specific about what you would actually say or do.
