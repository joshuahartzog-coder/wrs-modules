Work ethic: what employers actually measure

L1 Foundations 45–60 min block Standard 1.1.1 — Positive Work Ethic
Suggested timing: Warm-up 5 min · Instruction 15 min · Activity 20 min · Quiz + reflection 10 min

🕐 Warm-up · 5 min — Why work ethic is the foundation

Before we get into technical skills, safety procedures, or career ladders, every employer — regardless of industry — is asking the same basic questions about you:

  • Will you show up?
  • Will you do what you're asked?
  • Will you stay focused and follow through?

These questions describe work ethic, and they are not soft questions. They are the filter that determines whether you keep a job, get promoted, or get let go.

Nevada's Workplace Readiness Standards define a positive work ethic as: coming to work every day on time, a willingness to take direction, and motivation to accomplish the task at hand (Standard 1.1.1).

Industry voice: In surveys of Nevada employers, "reliability and attendance" and "following directions" consistently rank among the top three things employers say new hires struggle with most — above technical skills gaps.
🙋 Turn & Talk — 3 min

Before moving on, discuss with a partner:

  • Which of the three components do you think most students your age struggle with — and why?
  • Think of a job or team you've been part of. Which component mattered most in that environment?

Be ready to share one idea with the class.

📖 Instruction · 15 min — Work ethic is not just about effort

A common misconception: work ethic means working harder than everyone else. That's not quite right. Work ethic is about consistency, reliability, and responsiveness — not just effort or output.

Read both scenarios below, then continue:

Jordan works extremely hard when at work and produces excellent results. However, Jordan calls in sick frequently, often arrives a few minutes late, and sometimes responds to supervisor feedback with "I know, but my way works better." Jordan has not been promoted in two years despite strong output.

Taylor is not the highest performer on the team but is always on time, never misses a shift without advance notice, accepts feedback openly, and consistently completes every assigned task before leaving. Taylor was promoted to shift lead after one year.

Taylor's promotion wasn't about raw talent — it was about being dependable. Employers will invest in developing Taylor's skills because they trust Taylor to show up and follow through.

💬 Scenario debrief — 5 min

After reading both scenarios, discuss as a class or with a partner:

  • Jordan has better results than Taylor, but Taylor got promoted. Is that fair? Why or why not?
  • What specifically would Jordan need to change to be considered for promotion?
  • Have you ever worked with — or been — a Jordan or a Taylor? What happened?
