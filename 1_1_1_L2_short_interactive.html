Work ethic: reading employer expectations accurately

L2 Applied Short module · 8–12 min Standard 1.1.1 — Positive Work Ethic Use: Opener / closer / exam prep

You know what work ethic is. This module is about getting better at reading situations — which is exactly what the WRS exam tests. The exam doesn't ask you to define work ethic. It gives you a scenario and asks you to identify the right professional response.

Work ethic questions on the WRS often involve a conflict between personal preference and workplace expectation. The correct answer almost always prioritizes the workplace standard — not because your preferences don't matter, but because that is what professional behavior requires.

The judgment layer

At the L2 level, the question isn't just "does this person have good work ethic?" — it's "what does a professional do when work ethic is tested?" Consider these harder situations:

  • You are asked to do a task you think is inefficient. Do you say something, and if so, how and when?
  • A coworker's emergency means you're asked to cover work that wasn't yours. What's the professional response?
  • You believe you're more productive with music on, but company policy prohibits it. What do you do?

In each case, work ethic means following the professional standard — and if you disagree, raising it through the right channel at the right time, not ignoring the policy or pushing back in the moment.

Exam-style scenario — try it first

Read the scenario, pick your answer mentally, then reveal the explanation.

Chad discovered a quicker and safer way to complete a work process. However, his new approach does not follow the company's standard operating procedure.

What should Chad do?

  1. Use his new process to stand out from peers
  2. Share his idea with management before using it
  3. Drop the idea and follow the existing procedure
  4. Encourage coworkers to use his approach
Answer: B. Work ethic includes respecting systems and raising improvements through proper channels — not acting unilaterally, even with good intentions. C sounds safe but isn't quite right either: good employees do raise better ideas — just not by implementing them without approval.

⭐ Optional extender — if you finish early
Keisha is a second-year culinary arts student interning at a Las Vegas hotel kitchen. Her executive chef instructs her to prep vegetables a specific way. Keisha learned a different technique at school that she believes is faster and produces less waste. What should she do?
Answer: B. This is a classic L2 work ethic scenario — Keisha follows the professional standard in the moment, then raises her idea through the right channel at the right time. A deviates from the authorized method unilaterally. C bypasses the chain of command. D is a direct challenge to the chef's authority that will damage the relationship and her reputation before she's earned any standing to push back.
