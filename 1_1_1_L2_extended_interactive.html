Work ethic at the professional level: judgment under pressure

L2 Applied 45–60 min extended Standard 1.1.1 — Positive Work Ethic
Suggested timing: Review + framing 10 min · Employer perspective activity 15 min · Case studies 15 min · Quiz 10 min · Debrief 5 min

🕐 Review + Framing · 10 min — Moving beyond the basics

You covered work ethic in your Level 1 course. You know the three components: attendance and punctuality, willingness to take direction, and motivation to accomplish the task. At the L2 level, we're not reviewing those definitions — we're applying them to harder situations.

The real test of work ethic isn't how you behave when conditions are easy. It's how you behave when:

  • You're asked to do something you disagree with
  • A coworker's failure becomes your problem
  • The workplace standard conflicts with your personal approach
  • You believe you know a better way

Employers don't just want workers who follow rules when it's convenient. They want workers who can exercise professional judgment consistently — especially when it's inconvenient.

Industry voice: Nevada employers in healthcare, construction, hospitality, and logistics all report the same pattern — new hires who struggle most are not those who lack technical skills, but those who cannot follow direction under pressure or adapt when conditions change unexpectedly. "We can teach the job," one Reno manufacturing supervisor noted. "We can't teach someone to show up and take feedback."

Work ethic questions at this level often involve a dilemma with a "tempting wrong" answer — usually an option that sounds reasonable or even principled but bypasses the professional standard. Watch for options like "confront the person directly," "do it your way since it's better," or "involve a third party immediately." These usually signal an escalation error.

🗂 Case Studies · 15 min — Applied scenarios

Work through each case study with your group. Click Discussion notes when you're ready to compare your thinking.

Priya is a second-year medical assistant intern. Her supervisor has asked her to follow a documentation procedure that she knows from her training is outdated — the newer method is faster and produces fewer errors. Her supervisor seems resistant to change.

Discuss with your group:

  1. What should Priya do in the short term — today, during her shift?
  2. How could she raise her concern professionally without undermining her supervisor?
  3. What would the work ethic failure look like in this situation?
Key ideas to surface: In the short term, Priya should follow the current procedure — that's the professional standard while the change isn't approved. She can raise it by asking for a meeting, framing her concern as a question ("I read about a newer protocol — would it be worth reviewing?"), and bringing data. The work ethic failure would be implementing the new method unilaterally, even with good intentions — that's an unauthorized deviation that undermines the system and her supervisor's authority.

Marcus works at an auto shop. His coworker Devon called in sick on the busiest day of the week, and Marcus is now expected to cover Devon's bay in addition to his own work. Marcus already works hard and feels this is unfair. He has an important personal appointment after work.

Discuss with your group:

  1. What does professional work ethic look like in this situation?
  2. What are the limits? Is there a point at which Marcus's personal needs are legitimate?
  3. How should Marcus communicate with his supervisor about the situation?
Key ideas to surface: Professionally, Marcus covers the work — that's the expectation when a teammate is out. His frustration is valid, but airing it in the moment is not productive. The appointment is a real concern: if it's a medical or urgent obligation, Marcus should communicate it to his supervisor clearly and early ("I'll cover as much as I can, but I have a committed appointment at 5 — can we discuss coverage for the last hour?"). The work ethic failure would be refusing to cover, complaining publicly, or leaving without communication.
