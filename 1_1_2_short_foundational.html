Integrity: the five behaviors that build trust at work

Foundational Short Module · 8–12 min Standard 1.1.2 — Integrity Use: Opener / closer / exam prep

Integrity means doing the right thing — even when no one is watching, even when it's inconvenient, even when you could get away with not doing it. In the workplace, integrity shows up in five specific ways: honesty, accountability, ethical decision-making, trustworthiness, and confidentiality.

Integrity questions on the WRS often describe a situation where the "easy" option involves some form of dishonesty — covering up a mistake, going along with a coworker's deception, or overstating your qualifications. The correct answer almost always involves transparency and accountability, even when it's uncomfortable. Watch for distractors that frame dishonesty as loyalty, efficiency, or kindness.

The five dimensions of integrity

  • Honesty — telling the truth even when it's uncomfortable. This includes not exaggerating, not minimizing, and not letting silence create a false impression.
  • Accountability — owning your mistakes. Not deflecting, not blaming others, not minimizing — owning the outcome and contributing to the fix.
  • Ethical decision-making — choosing the right action over the convenient one. This often means acting without anyone asking you to.
  • Trustworthiness — doing what you say you'll do. Reliability. Following through even when it becomes inconvenient.
  • Confidentiality — protecting information that isn't yours to share. Personnel matters, business decisions, client information — if it's sensitive, it stays with you.

Exam-style scenario — try it first

Jordan overhears his supervisor tell a colleague that the company may be laying off two people in the next quarter. Jordan's coworker, Mia, asks him later: "Have you heard anything about layoffs? I'm worried." Jordan knows something but isn't sure it's accurate or finalized.

What should Jordan do?

Jordan should not share what he overheard. The information was not shared with him directly, may be incomplete, and is not his to pass along. Sharing it could cause unnecessary anxiety, damage trust, and create problems if the information was inaccurate. The professional response is: "I don't have any information about that — I'd talk to your supervisor if you're concerned." Confidentiality applies to information you overhear, not just information formally shared with you.

⭐ Optional extender — if you finish early
You're working a shift and notice a coworker taking an item from the supply room without logging it — something the policy requires. It's a small item. The coworker isn't doing anything malicious. What's the professional response, and does it change if this is a pattern?
One time, minor item: A quiet, friendly mention is appropriate — "Hey, just a heads-up, you're supposed to log that." You're not policing; you're being a good colleague. If it's a pattern: The calculus changes. A pattern of small thefts is still theft, and staying silent makes you complicit. The professional step is to mention it to the coworker directly first, then escalate if the behavior continues. Your obligation to integrity doesn't disappear because the value of the item is small.
